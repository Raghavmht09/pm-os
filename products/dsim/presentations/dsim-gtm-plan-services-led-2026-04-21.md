## CS Enablement

**Top 10 customer questions and suggested answers:**

1. **Q: How is this different from what Pacvue/CommerceIQ already gives us?**
   → A: Pacvue and CommerceIQ measure incrementality inside their own activation engine — the goal is to optimize bids, not to give you a vendor-neutral number you can take to your CFO. DSIM has no activation stake. It's designed to be the independent audit layer, not the activation layer.

2. **Q: We already have an MMM — Analytic Partners or Circana. Why do we need DSIM?**
   → A: Traditional MMMs are quarterly, don't model digital shelf signals (availability, content, SoS) as first-class inputs, and don't decompose at store-cluster level. DSIM is the always-on, shelf-native complement — think of it as what feeds into your MMM, not what replaces it.

3. **Q: Do we need to be a DataWeave DSA customer to use DSIM?**
   → A: No. DSIM can ingest shelf data from Profitero, NielsenIQ, or Stackline. Walmart Connect API data and your sales data (Luminate or equivalent) are the core requirements.

4. **Q: What data do we need to provide?**
   → A: Three input streams: (1) Walmart shelf KPIs — DataWeave DSA or alternative source (Profitero, NielsenIQ); (2) Walmart Connect campaign data — ad spend, impressions, clicks by campaign via Walmart Connect API; (3) Walmart Scintilla (formerly Luminate) — if you have Scintilla access, the Channel Performance module covers your sales data, Digital Landscapes supplements shelf signals, and Insights Activation supplements media data. One Scintilla connection covers most of stream 3. If you don't have Scintilla, we can work with internal sales exports. Pre-qualifying your Scintilla module access is step one of scoping.

5. **Q: How long does it take to get the first output?**
   → A: Target is 10 weeks from data onboarding complete to first dashboard delivery. Lactalis PoC was 13 weeks including model iteration.

6. **Q: How accurate is the model?**
   → A: Lactalis PoC achieved 91% R² and 5.9% MAPE. Industry-standard MMM MAPE benchmarks range from 10–15%. We target ≤ 8% MAPE floor on every engagement.

7. **Q: What does the deliverable actually look like?**
   → A: Tableau dashboard (shared with your team) plus a quarterly executive presentation following the format of our Lactalis final deck — decomposition by sub-brand, iROI by channel, availability impact analysis, budget reallocation recommendations, and store-cluster view.

8. **Q: How is pricing structured?**
   → A: PoC engagement (10 weeks, 1 brand, Walmart-only) starts at $50K–$75K. Annual ongoing engagement is $150K–$200K per brand portfolio. Multi-brand enterprise pricing available.

9. **Q: Can this be extended to other retailers like Amazon or Target?**
   → A: Walmart is our launch retailer. Amazon and Target are on the roadmap for Q4 2026. We want to be excellent at Walmart before expanding — Walmart-native store-cluster modeling is where our differentiation is sharpest.

10. **Q: If we want to expand to more sub-brands, what does that cost?**
    → A: Sub-brand expansion is included in enterprise pricing. Adding a new sub-brand mid-engagement has incremental data onboarding cost — discuss with CS lead.

### Q1 — Leading vs. Lagging Metrics: ICP Validation

**The core metric chain DSIM operates on:**

Leading metrics (shelf + media signals DSIM models) → Lagging metrics (business outcomes brands report to CFO):

| Leading Metric | What It Measures | Lagging Metric It Drives | Validated Relationship |
|---|---|---|---|
| On-shelf availability (OSA) | % of time product is in stock and purchasable | Walmart.com unit sales | Industry benchmark: 3% OSA improvement ≈ 1% sales increase (Quri study) |

| Budget allocation efficiency | % of spend in high-iROI vs. low-iROI channels | Marketing contribution margin | Lactalis: 63% of budget in 0.83 iROI channel; 22% in 1.55 iROI channel |

**Validating the problem statement with ICP brands — 3 qualifying questions for the scoping call:**

### Q2 — Value-Based Reverse Pricing: Impact-Linked Slab Model

The engagement-type pricing in this plan is a starting structure, not the target model. The correct long-term architecture is impact-linked pricing, built from the value delivered backward to the fee. Here is how to structure it:

**Step 1 — Define "DSIM Opportunity Value" as the pricing anchor:**

At the end of every DSIM engagement, the deliverable produces a quantified opportunity in three buckets:
- **Budget reallocation delta**: $ of annual spend currently in channels with iROI < 1.0 that could shift to channels with iROI > 1.5
- **Availability revenue recovery**: $ of incremental annual sales if OSA improves from current rate to 88%+ (using the 3% OSA → 1% sales benchmark applied to brand's Walmart GMV)
- **Halo effect credit**: $ of sub-brand sales currently uncredited or misattributed

Lactalis benchmark: ~$10.9M/year identified opportunity ($30K/day). DSIM fee at 1.5–2% of identified opportunity = $165K–$220K/year — which is exactly where our Annual Engagement pricing sits. This proves the model works directionally.

**Step 2 — Slab structure tied to identified opportunity:**

| Tier | Annual DSIM Opportunity Identified | DataWeave Annual Fee | Logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Starter | Up to $1M identified | $75K–$100K | Minimum viable engagement; smaller brands or single-category scope |
| Core | $1M – $3M identified | $150K–$200K | Standard 1-brand, Walmart-only engagement |
| Growth | $3M – $7M identified | $250K–$350K | Multi-sub-brand; portfolio-level halo effects visible |
| Enterprise | $7M+ identified | $400K–$600K+ | Multi-brand parent company; Scintilla full-suite; quarterly + event-triggered refresh |

**Step 3 — How the slab escalates at renewal (the user's insight on data volume growth):**

The slab moves up at annual renewal when:
- Brand adds sub-brands → more Scintilla data, more PCA factors, more model compute
- Brand adds retailers (Q4 2026 expansion) → additional data pipeline + model calibration
- Opportunity identified in Year 1 exceeds the tier ceiling → pricing steps up for Year 2

This aligns DataWeave's fee growth with brand value growth — the more DSIM proves out, the more the brand invests across their portfolio, and the more DataWeave charges. It's a natural expansion motion built into the contract.

**Important caveat for Phase 0 (services-led now):** The slab model requires a defined measurement methodology for "verified impact" — which is what the engagement deliverable produces but not what gets tracked post-delivery in the services model. For now: anchor PoC pricing to the Lactalis benchmark ROI story, commit to a Year 1 opportunity quantification in the final deck, and use that number to set Year 2 renewal pricing at the right slab. Full performance-linked pricing (pay-for-impact) is a Phase 1+ product motion, not a services motion.

### Q4 — Competitive Deep Dive: Activation-Led vs. Diagnostic Incrementality (+ New Competitor Alert)

**First: a significant competitive gap from live research — Incremental.com.**

Incremental.com is an official Walmart Connect measurement partner (announced April 2025), WPP Media's first "agency Connected Partner," and purpose-built for retail media incrementality measurement with no activation stake. They are the closest match to DSIM's measurement-only positioning in the market today. They were absent from our competitive landscape doc. This needs to be added.

**DSIM vs. Incremental.com differentiation:**
- Incremental.com: cross-retailer (100+ RMNs), campaign-level measurement, media-only model, no shelf-signal integration
- DSIM: Walmart-native, shelf signals as first-class inputs (availability, SoS, content), store-cluster localization, Scintilla integration, Robyn + GAM + PCA with published MAPE/R²

The dividing line in the full competitive map is now three-way, not two-way:

| | Activation-Led (CIQ, Pacvue) | Campaign Measurement (Incremental.com, Skai) | Shelf-Native Diagnostic (DSIM) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary buyer | Media ops / retail media manager | Analytics / marketing finance | RGM / Finance / C-suite |
| Core question | "How do I optimize my bids right now?" | "Did this campaign generate net-new sales?" | "Which shelf + media lever drove incremental sales — and which were suppressed by availability?" |
| Cadence | Real-time / daily | Campaign-level / post-period | Always-on, quarterly minimum |
| Shelf signals as inputs | No | No | Yes — first-class inputs |
| Store-cluster resolution | No | No | Yes (17 clusters in Lactalis) |
| Methodology published | No | No | Yes (5.9% MAPE, 91% R²) |
| Activation conflict | Yes | No | No |
| Walmart-specific depth | Medium | Medium (official partner) | Deep (Walmart Connect + Scintilla + DSA) |

**The fundamental use case difference in plain terms:**

CommerceIQ and Pacvue solve the *execution problem*: "My campaign is running — make it perform better, faster." The model feeds the bid engine. Incrementality is an input, not the output. Their buyer is the person who logs in daily and makes campaign adjustments.

Incremental.com solves the *attribution problem*: "My campaign ran — how much was truly incremental?" Their model is post-period, campaign-scoped, media-only. Their buyer is the analytics team that writes the campaign debrief. They can tell you iROAS for one campaign, but they cannot tell you how availability rates constrained that campaign or what the halo effect was across sub-brands.

DSIM solves the *strategy problem*: "Across all my Walmart levers — media, shelf, content, pricing — what drove my sales this quarter and how should I allocate budget differently next quarter to maximize incremental return?" Their buyer is the RGM team that sets annual Walmart strategy and has to defend it to the CFO and in the JBP. The output is a strategic reallocation recommendation, not a campaign report.

The summary one-liner per competitor:
- CommerceIQ / Pacvue: *"We'll optimize your campaigns for you."*
- Incremental.com / Skai: *"We'll measure whether your campaigns worked."*
- DataWeave DSIM: *"We'll tell you which of your 6 shelf levers drove your Walmart sales — and what to change across media AND shelf to make next quarter better."*

### Q5 — Native Product Dashboard: From Tableau to Productized DSIM

Yes — once DSIM is productized inside DataWeave's native platform (Phase 2), the Tableau template is retired and replaced with a native dashboard. This is a significant UX and architecture discussion. Here is the recommended design framework to guide it.

**Three user types → three dashboard views:**

| User | Job to Be Done | Primary View |
|---|---|---|
| VP RGM / CMO | Defend Walmart budget to CFO; set annual JBP allocation | **Executive Summary**: one-page impact decomposition — total iROI, top 3 recommendations, budget reallocation waterfall |
| Walmart Channel Manager / Analytics Director | Diagnose shelf performance weekly; identify which cluster is underperforming | **Analyst View**: sub-brand decomposition, lever-by-lever breakdown, store-cluster map, availability × media correlation chart |
| Brand's Media Agency | Export DSIM outputs into activation tools to adjust campaigns | **Agency Export View**: API feed or CSV export of iROI by channel, by cluster, by period |

**Core modules for the native DSIM dashboard:**

1. **Impact Decomposition Waterfall** — Total Walmart sales broken into: Baseline + Availability impact + SoS impact + Content impact + Onsite Search lift + Offsite Display lift + Halo Effect + Residual. This is the Lactalis final deck slide 1 made always-on and interactive.

2. **iROI by Channel Bubble Chart** — X-axis = current spend, Y-axis = iROI, bubble size = attributed incremental sales. One view shows immediately where spend is misallocated. Reallocation simulator: drag bubble to "optimal" zone and see projected impact.

3. **Store Cluster Map** — 17 clusters (configurable) heat-mapped by over/under-performance vs. national average. Drill into any cluster to see availability rate, SoS, media spend, and iROI. Action layer: flag clusters where availability < threshold with "fix before media" alert.

4. **Availability × Media Correlation Panel** — Time series chart: OSA rate (left axis) overlaid with media spend and sales (right axis). Makes the Lactalis finding ("78% in-stock during peak media") visible at a glance. Direct visual proof of the core DSIM insight.

5. **Budget Reallocation Recommendation** — From/to table: current allocation vs. DSIM-optimized allocation, with projected incremental impact of the shift. Links directly to the agency export for activation.

6. **Scintilla & Data Freshness Panel** — Which modules are connected, last refresh date, any data gap alerts. Operationalizes the Scintilla integration visible to the user.

7. **Seasonal Alert Engine** — User sets thresholds (e.g., "alert me when cluster 7 OSA drops below 85%"). DSIM fires an estimated impact: "Cluster 7 OSA at 81% — estimated $X at risk in media ROI over next 4 weeks."

**Key distinction from DSA's dashboard:**
- DSA answers: *"What is happening on my shelf right now?"* — operational, daily, execution-oriented
- DSIM answers: *"Why did my sales move last quarter, and what should I change for next quarter?"* — strategic, quarterly, budget-allocation-oriented
- They share the same shelf data underneath and should live inside the same DataWeave platform — the brand uses DSA daily and opens DSIM quarterly for the strategic view

**Phase sequencing to get there:**
- Phase 0 (now): Tableau template; CS + DS deliver manually
- Phase 1 deliverable: DSIM Setup Wizard + automated model run; still output to Tableau; build the results data store (the API layer) behind the scenes
- Phase 2 deliverable: Build the native dashboard on top of the Phase 1 results store; Tableau deprecated; agency export API goes live; alert engine added
- Phase 3 (optional): Reallocation simulator with live Walmart Connect bid integration — this is where DSIM and activation start to converge, but on DataWeave's terms, not CommerceIQ's

The results data store built in Phase 1 is the architectural prerequisite that makes Phase 2 possible. Do not skip it.
